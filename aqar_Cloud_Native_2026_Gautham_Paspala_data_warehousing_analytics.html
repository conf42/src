Cloud-Native Friendly Fraud Detection with Temporal Graph Attention and Transformers

Video size:

Abstract

First-party (friendly) fraud is one of the fastest-growing threats in payments, with industry estimates attributing 60–75% of chargeback volume to friendly fraud and $48B+ in annual global losses. Unlike third-party fraud, the transaction is often legitimate at purchase time, while fraud occurs later through dispute filing. This temporal disconnect limits point-of-transaction controls and helps explain why rule-based systems typically capture only 23–31% of confirmed friendly fraud cases. Traditional machine learning improves detection to approximately 38–47%, but remains constrained by feature engineering and limited ability to model networked behavior across accounts, devices, merchants, and time. This talk proposes an attention-driven detection framework that combines heterogeneous temporal transaction graphs with transformer-based behavioral sequence modeling. Payment ecosystems are represented as large-scale graphs spanning cardholders, transactions, merchants, devices, IPs, and addresses, at issuer-scale volumes: 50–100M accounts, 15–25M merchants, and graphs reaching 10–50B edges, with 100–500M edges added daily. Graph attention highlights suspicious structural relationships (such as device-sharing or repeated cross-merchant patterns), while transformer self-attention captures sequential signals such as dispute targeting behavior across time windows. The session outlines how these architectures can be evaluated using fraud detection metrics such as precision, recall, AUC, and cost-sensitive threshold optimization, benchmarked against rule-based and traditional ML baselines. It also discusses deployment considerations relevant to cloud-native systems, including scalable graph construction, segment-level calibration, and human-in-the-loop review workflows to reduce false positives and prevent customer friction in real-world payment environments.
