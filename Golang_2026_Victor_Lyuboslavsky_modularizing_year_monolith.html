Modularizing a 10-Year Monolith: The Architecture, The People, and the Pain

Video size:

Abstract

Most Go codebases begin as straightforward layered monoliths, but years of growth often turn those layers into a web of hidden coupling, unclear ownership, and hard-to-predict side effects. Small changes start requiring deep context, and compile and test times slowly creep up, turning routine work into risky work. Rewrites promise a clean slate but rarely succeed in practice. What the Go community lacks are real examples of large open source Go projects that have successfully evolved toward a modular monolith. This talk presents a major open source Go project in the middle of that evolution. It covers how we are moving from a decade-old layered architecture toward a modular design while continuing to ship features to multiple production environments that teams actively depend on every day. This is not theory. This is architectural change in a live system, with real contributors, long CI pipelines, social constraints, and legacy assumptions embedded throughout the code. What began as an investigation into slow build and test times exposed deeper problems: oversized packages, uncontrolled dependencies, unclear boundaries, and an architecture that no longer matched how engineers actually reasoned about the system. We walk through familiar pain points in large Go monoliths, including tight coupling, long feedback cycles, and frequent merge conflicts, and explain why these problems persist even in well-intentioned codebases. You’ll see where our early attempts stalled, how architectural changes regressed quietly over time, why good ideas alone were not enough, and how steady, incremental refactoring helped us regain control without freezing development. Architecture only works when people agree to carry it together. Whether you are working on a fast-growing Go project or maintaining a mature production system, this talk will give you concrete techniques and mental models for evolving your architecture safely. You’ll leave with a clearer understanding of how to introduce boundaries that hold, align ownership with code, and make a large system feel smaller, safer, and easier to change, so teams can move faster without needing to hold the entire system in their heads.

It was forcing the system to say more precisely what the behavior actually was. Another strong signal that this approach works came from something ordinary. This internal package, internal directory. Code outside that bound- boundary simply cannot import the implementation. The boundary is this whole directory, the se- service activity directory. So in Go, internal is enforced by the compiler. If you could put code inside it, nothing outside this part of the code base can import it. No conventions, no code review rules, no "Please don't do this," it just fails to compile. Why I bring this up is because a lot of architectural boundaries are softer than they look. They depend on engineers remembering the rules, reviewers noticing violations, and everyone continuing to agree on the structure. Internal removes that entire class of failure. It means another bounded context cannot reach into your service implementation just because it's convenient. It cannot pull your MySQL layer directly. It can't quietly start depending on internals that were supposed to stay private. The compiler stops it, and in a large code base, that's a big difference. At that po- point, the boundary is no longer just something written in an ADR or drawn on a slide, it is part of the program itself. You can't cross it accidentally. Another pattern that proved useful was the bootstrap layer over here. Each bounded context has a single entry point, a bootstrap.new function. That's the only place where dependencies are wired together. From the outside, you don't see the internal structure, you don't know about the database implementation of the service internals, you just get a fully constructed Bounded context service. That sounds small, but it simplifies a lot of things. Testing becomes easier, integration becomes predictable, and r- it reinforces the idea that a bounded context is a unit, not just a collection of packages. It gives us one consistent way to instantiate the bounded context in both production and tests. That was a bigger deal than I expected. Without a single composition root, dependency wiring tends to spread across the cal- codebase. Different entry points start assembling slightly different versions of the same thing, and then it becomes harder to tell whe- whether different environments are really exercising the same code path. Keeping that wiring in one place removed a lot of that ambiguity. We introduced an anti-corruption layer between the new context and the legacy system. Now, we covered this part earlier, and this was the first time we applied it in this codebase. With the ACL, those calls moved behind a single interface. That interface listed exactly what the new bounded context depended on, right? We could point to methods, not categories or concepts, actual functions. Most of them already existed in a legacy system, but we also had to add a couple new methods to support this interface. That addition was useful. It forced us to be explicit about a dependency that would have otherwise stayed implicit. At that point, the ACL became easier to reason about. If you want to understand how this context interacts with the rest of the system, you just read this interface. And it also gives us a simple way to track progress. Every method in this ACL is a dependency on the legacy system. Fewer methods means less coupling. More methods means the boundary still needs some work. That interface gave us a much clearer definition of done. The goal was not to build an impressive ACL, the goal was keep that interface narrow. If the activity context needed five or six methods from legacy code, fine. If it kept growing, that was a signal. Either the boundary was wrong or we were still dragging too much of the monolith through that seam. In that sense, the ACL was not just a bridge, it was a measurement point. We also changed how CI, continuous integration, works for this code. Instead of running everything on every change, the activity context has its own workflow, its own CI workflow. It only runs when files in that context change. That had an effect on both sides of the system For the new bounded context, the signal is clear. If something fails, it is in the activity-bounded context. There's no ambiguity about where to look. For the legacy code base, it also helped. We removed activity-related tests from the main test suite, so the remaining tests run slightly faster, and there's less noise from unrelated failures. That separation is small but noticeable, and more importantly, it reflects the architecture. The code is split, and now the feedback loops are split as well. That sounds operational, but it changed behavior. When the team sees a failure tied to its own context, ownership is clear, and when unrelated failures stop showing up in the same workflow, people spend less time triaging noise. The architecture becomes visible not just in the code layout, but in the daily developer loop. We also added architecture tests. They define which packages are allowed to import which. Each bounded context has an allow list. If a package pulls in something outside that list, the test fails. That check runs in CI just like any other test. So if someone or an AI agent introduces a new dependency across a boundary, it shows up immediately. There's no need to catch it in a review, and it doesn't rely on people remembering the rules. The constraint is in the code. Taken together, internal, the ACL, the bootstrap layer, the dedicated CI workflow, and the architecture tests did something important. They turned the bounded context from simply a clean directory structure into something we could enforce, test, and operate separately. That is a much stronger result than, "The code looks nicer." But it's important to be clear about what this pilot did not solve. We did not modularize the system. We modularized a small part of it. Most of the monolith is still there. Most boundaries are still undefined. We still have shared concepts that don't map cleanly to a single context, and we still have open questions about how these contexts will interact as the system grows. So this is not a finished architecture. It is a validated direction. Where we are now is somewhere in the middle. We've completed one bounded context, and now we're figuring out how to do the next ones. The next likely candidates are user management and certificate management, and those are harder. Activity was a good pilot because it was more self-contained. User management and certificate management sit closer to the center of the system. They touch more workflows, more teams, and more legacy code. So the next phase is not just repeating what we already did, it is applying the same ideas in parts of the monolith where the boundaries are less obvious and the coupling is heavier. But the uncertainty is different now. We're no longer asking whether this can work. We've seen it work. Now the problem is scaling it carefully without breaking what already exists. All right. Let's circle back to the core problem we're trying to solve. Looking back, the real constraint wasn't Go. It wasn't MySQL. It wasn't even the monolith. The real constraint was human attention. How much of the system can one people hold in their head? How much confidently can they make a change? And how much unrelated context they have to load just to get started? Once a system grows beyond that limit, progress slows, no matter how good the engineers are. We often talk about architecture as if it's for computers. But computers don't care how big your code base is. Humans do. Architecture is really an interface for people. It defines what you need to understand, what you can safely ignore, and what you're allowed to change. When that interface is unclear, every change feels risky. When it's clear, people move faster, even in a large system. The tricky part is that this problem shows up after success. The monolith works, the product ships, customers are happy, and slowly, almost invisibly, the system becomes harder to change. We're not fixing a broken system. We're fixing a system that worked until it didn't. That's why these conversations are hard, and that's why they matter. We knew our system had grown too complex. Not complex in an abstract sense, complex in a human sense. It had grown beyond what a human brain could comfortably hold. Every small change required understanding too much at once. At that point, adding rules or documentation doesn't fix the problem. You have to change the shape of the system. For a long time, I thought the hard part was architecture, or code, or tooling. It wasn't. The hardest part was alignment with other humans. Shared language, shared understanding, shared ownership. Code changes fast, especially now with AI agents. Architecture survives only when people agree to carry it together. We don't have a clean ending. The work is still moving. Boundaries are still being uncovered. Edge cases are still surfacing. But something important has shifted. The system is starting to feel smaller, more defined, easier to follow. And for the first time in a long time, the code base is helping us think instead of getting in the way. So if there's one thing I hope you take away from this talk, it's this. Modularization isn't really about modules. It's about respecting the limits of human attention. It's about designing systems that people can understand, trust, and change without fear. Tools matter, patterns matter, but architecture lasts only when it's owned by more than one person. That's it for this talk. If this resonated with you, I'd be interested to hear from you. How are you approaching similar problems in your own systems? Details are different, but the patterns usually aren't. Here's a few links to Fleet, my company. This QR code goes to our webpage. We are hiring developers, Go developers. And then you can, there's links, you can find me. Feel free to connect with me on LinkedIn. Thank you for listening.
