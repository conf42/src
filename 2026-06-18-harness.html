Shipping with Context: Knowledge Graphs as the Backbone of AI-First Software Delivery

June 18 2026

Shipping with Context: Why Knowledge Graphs Are Becoming the Backbone of AI-First Software Delivery

AI is transforming software development at an extraordinary pace. Developers can generate code, write tests, create documentation, and troubleshoot issues faster than ever before.

Yet for most organizations, the real challenge is no longer generating code.

The harder problem is understanding context.

A piece of generated code does not exist in isolation. It interacts with services, infrastructure, security controls, deployment pipelines, compliance requirements, operational processes, and business objectives. Without understanding those relationships, AI systems may be capable of producing outputs, but they struggle to make reliable decisions.

In his session Shipping with Context: Knowledge Graphs as the Backbone of AI-First Software Delivery, Prateek Mittal explored why context is emerging as the next critical layer of enterprise AI and why knowledge graphs are becoming a foundational component of modern software delivery systems.

The Next AI Bottleneck Is Not Model Capability

Over the past few years, the conversation around AI has largely focused on model performance.

Organizations compare models, benchmark reasoning capabilities, and evaluate new generations of large language models.

However, many enterprises are discovering that model quality is no longer the primary limitation.

The real bottleneck is access to organizational context.

Most enterprises operate across dozens or hundreds of systems:

  • CI/CD platforms
  • Security tools
  • Cloud infrastructure
  • Observability platforms
  • Ticketing systems
  • Source control repositories
  • Incident management platforms
  • Cost management tools

Each system contains valuable information.

The challenge is that this information remains fragmented.

An AI assistant may understand how to write code, but it often lacks visibility into which services are affected by that code, which teams own those services, what policies apply to them, and what business risks might be introduced by a deployment.

Without context, intelligence remains limited.

Why Enterprise AI Struggles With Context

The problem is not simply a lack of data.

Most organizations already have enormous amounts of operational information available.

The challenge is that this information exists inside disconnected systems.

As Mittal explained, enterprise AI initiatives frequently encounter four common obstacles.

Data Silos

Critical information is distributed across numerous platforms and tools.

Even when the data exists, it is often inaccessible to AI systems or difficult to connect across domains.

Missing Domain Knowledge

General-purpose language models understand public information but typically lack awareness of company-specific terminology, workflows, ownership structures, and operational practices.

This creates a gap between what AI knows and what an organization actually needs.

Hallucination Risk

When models lack context, they compensate by making assumptions.

These assumptions can produce inaccurate recommendations, incorrect conclusions, or actions based on incomplete information.

Governance and Compliance Concerns

Organizations must carefully control how sensitive information is accessed and shared.

Providing AI systems with unrestricted access to enterprise data can introduce security, compliance, and governance risks.

Together, these challenges limit the ability of AI systems to operate effectively in production environments.

The Evolution From AI-Assisted DevOps to AI-Operational DevOps

Many teams are already using AI in their software delivery processes.

Most of these implementations fall into what Mittal describes as AI-assisted DevOps.

In this model, AI helps humans perform tasks such as:

  • Writing code
  • Creating configuration files
  • Generating documentation
  • Producing runbooks
  • Answering operational questions

Humans remain responsible for supplying context, validating outputs, and making final decisions.

This approach delivers productivity gains, but it does not fundamentally change how software delivery operates.

A second model is beginning to emerge.

What Is AI-Operational DevOps?

AI-operational DevOps shifts AI from being a productivity tool to becoming an active participant in delivery workflows.

Instead of requiring humans to manually provide context, AI systems automatically retrieve information from shared enterprise models.

This allows agents to:

  • Evaluate deployment risks
  • Analyze dependencies
  • Simulate outcomes
  • Enforce policies
  • Validate compliance requirements
  • Recommend corrective actions

In this model, context becomes machine-readable rather than conversational.

The system itself becomes responsible for understanding relationships and applying guardrails before actions occur.

This transition requires a shared understanding of the software delivery ecosystem.

That is where knowledge graphs enter the picture.

Understanding the Building Blocks of a Knowledge Graph

A knowledge graph is not simply a database or reporting system.

It is a representation of entities and the relationships between them.

Within a software delivery environment, entities may include:

  • Services
  • Pipelines
  • Deployments
  • Incidents
  • Infrastructure resources
  • Cloud costs
  • Security findings
  • Teams
  • Applications

These entities become nodes within the graph.

The relationships between them become edges.

For example:

  • A service depends on another service
  • A deployment originates from a specific commit
  • A pipeline belongs to a particular team
  • An incident affects a specific application
  • A cloud resource supports a particular workload

Individually, each piece of information has limited value.

Connected together, they create a model that describes how an organization actually operates.

Context Is More Than Data

One of the key ideas presented during the session is that context is not merely information.

Context is information combined with relationships.

Many organizations already collect deployment events, operational metrics, cloud costs, and security findings.

The challenge is that these data points often remain disconnected.

A knowledge graph introduces a semantic layer that allows systems to understand how information relates across domains.

This transforms isolated records into an interconnected model of software delivery.

Instead of querying a single tool, AI systems can reason across multiple systems simultaneously.

Building a Knowledge Graph for Software Delivery

Creating a useful knowledge graph requires more than importing data.

Organizations must establish a process for constructing and maintaining relationships.

The process generally includes several stages.

Entity Extraction

The first step is identifying important objects within the delivery ecosystem.

These may include applications, services, incidents, deployments, users, cloud resources, or security findings.

Metadata Enrichment

Entities become more valuable when supplemented with additional information.

Examples include:

  • Ownership details
  • Team assignments
  • Version information
  • Environment data
  • Compliance classifications
  • Security policies

Relationship Mapping

Relationships define how entities interact.

Understanding who owns a service, which deployment modified it, and what infrastructure supports it is often more valuable than understanding any individual component in isolation.

Query Exposure

Once the graph exists, APIs and query interfaces allow AI agents to retrieve and reason about context dynamically.

At that point, the graph becomes an operational system rather than simply a data repository.

Why Knowledge Graph Projects Fail

Knowledge graphs have existed for years, yet many implementations struggle to deliver value.

According to Mittal, the most common failure is not technical.

It is a failure of use case definition.

Organizations sometimes invest heavily in building sophisticated graph models without clearly identifying the problems they intend to solve.

This creates several risks.

Over-Modeling

Teams create highly detailed ontologies that are technically impressive but provide little practical value.

The result is significant complexity with limited adoption.

Under-Modeling

The opposite problem occurs when relationships are not modeled deeply enough.

Data remains connected only superficially, preventing meaningful reasoning.

Stale Context

A graph is only valuable if it reflects reality.

Outdated relationships and stale metadata quickly reduce the usefulness of AI-driven decision-making.

Successful implementations focus on solving specific operational challenges rather than building graphs for their own sake.

How Knowledge Graphs Enable AI Reasoning

One of the most compelling aspects of knowledge graphs is their ability to support complex reasoning across multiple systems.

During the session, Mittal demonstrated how AI could analyze software delivery pipelines across an organization and identify patterns that would be difficult to discover manually.

Rather than searching individual projects one at a time, the system was able to reason across organizational boundaries.

The AI identified:

  • Pipelines with the highest failure rates
  • Common failure patterns
  • Missing dependencies
  • Configuration issues
  • Potential remediation actions

The system not only surfaced operational problems but also suggested corrective actions based on the relationships captured inside the graph.

This illustrates a broader shift in how AI interacts with enterprise systems.

Instead of responding only to direct questions, AI can begin to understand operational context and assist with diagnosis, prioritization, and decision-making.

Shared Context Across Engineering Disciplines

The value of a knowledge graph extends far beyond DevOps.

Modern software delivery requires collaboration across multiple domains.

DevOps teams care about deployments and reliability.

SecOps teams focus on vulnerabilities and compliance.

FinOps teams analyze cloud spending and resource utilization.

Platform engineering teams manage infrastructure and developer experience.

Traditionally, each group operates within its own set of tools and dashboards.

Knowledge graphs provide a shared context layer that connects these perspectives.

When everyone operates against the same model of services, dependencies, ownership, and operational events, decision-making becomes faster and more consistent.

Governance Becomes a Core AI Requirement

As AI systems gain access to broader organizational context, governance becomes increasingly important.

Enterprise AI cannot operate solely on trust.

Systems require mechanisms for:

  • Role-based access control
  • Policy enforcement
  • Auditability
  • Compliance validation
  • Permission management

Knowledge graphs provide the contextual foundation that allows these controls to be applied consistently.

Without governance, increased context introduces risk.

With governance, context becomes a strategic advantage.

The Future of AI-First Software Delivery

The software industry has spent years building increasingly capable AI models.

The next phase of adoption will depend less on model intelligence and more on organizational context.

The most successful AI systems will not necessarily be those with the largest parameter counts.

They will be the systems that understand services, dependencies, ownership, policies, infrastructure, incidents, and business priorities.

Knowledge graphs provide a mechanism for connecting these elements into a unified model.

As AI moves from assisting developers to actively participating in software delivery workflows, shared context will become essential infrastructure.

Context Is the New Competitive Advantage

Software delivery is becoming increasingly complex.

Organizations operate across dozens of platforms, hundreds of services, and thousands of interconnected components.

AI has the potential to help manage this complexity, but only if it can understand the environment in which it operates.

Knowledge graphs offer a way to transform fragmented organizational knowledge into a connected, queryable system that both humans and AI can reason about.

In an AI-first future, the question will no longer be whether models can generate answers.

The question will be whether they have enough context to generate the right ones.
