Engineering Production-Grade Multi-Agent Systems in the Cloud

Video size:

Abstract

Most organizations begin their AI journey with a single conversational interface. The architectural inflection point occurs when that interface evolves into a distributed, cloud-native system of collaborating agents executing real operational workflows. At this stage, complexity increases in ways that demos rarely expose: orchestration depth grows across services, latency compounds across agent hops, partial failures propagate through reasoning chains, and non-deterministic outputs introduce variability that must be governed rather than ignored. In this talk, I present the evolution of a production order-support platform that transitioned from a monolithic chatbot into a centrally orchestrated, multi-agent architecture deployed in a cloud-native environment. The redesign required explicit coordination control, isolation of failure domains to prevent cascading errors, and observability capable of reconstructing multi-hop reasoning paths across distributed components. The core challenge was not improving prompts, but engineering predictability, resilience, and trust in a system where autonomous agents collaborate. The session examines when orchestration provides advantages over decentralized agent swarms, how well-defined agent boundaries reduce contradictory outputs, and why observability must capture decision artifacts—not just infrastructure metrics. I will also discuss trade-offs between flexibility and determinism, and how governance becomes essential once agents influence production workflows. Attendees will leave with cloud-native architectural patterns and engineering principles for building resilient, traceable, and governable multi-agent systems under real-world workloads.
