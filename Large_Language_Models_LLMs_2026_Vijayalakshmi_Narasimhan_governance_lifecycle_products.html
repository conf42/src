Responsible AI Product Management for Production LLM Lifecycles

Video size:

Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are rapidly moving from experimentation into production systems, but many organizations still manage them using traditional Product Development Lifecycle (PDLC) practices designed for deterministic software. In real-world deployments, LLM systems introduce probabilistic behavior, evolving outputs, model drift, bias risks, limited explainability, and operational uncertainty that standard governance and product management approaches often fail to address. This session presents a practical Responsible AI Product Management framework for building, deploying, and operating LLM-based products across the PDLC lifecycle. Drawing from real-world product management and governance practices, the talk explains how teams can integrate ethics, compliance, trust, and operational controls into everyday AI delivery workflows without slowing innovation. The session covers how product managers can translate frameworks such as the OECD AI Principles and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework into actionable lifecycle controls. Topics include defining trust-aware requirements for fairness, explainability, and auditability; embedding compliance-by-design into product planning; and using governance artifacts such as Model Cards and Datasheets for Datasets to improve transparency and accountability. Attendees will also learn practical approaches for extending validation beyond traditional QA through bias testing, robustness evaluation, scenario-based validation, and continuous monitoring strategies that help detect model drift and changing user behavior after deployment. The talk highlights lessons learned from managing AI systems where reliability, governance, and user trust must evolve alongside the model itself. This session is designed for product managers, AI practitioners, and engineering leaders looking for practical methods to operationalize Responsible AI in production LLM systems while balancing business goals, regulatory expectations, and long-term system reliability.
